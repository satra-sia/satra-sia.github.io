Kawaii Colonisers

The Rebranding of Japan's Bloody Past

I want you to close your eyes and picture Japan. What is it that you see? I’m sure the visualisation of a foreign land is different for everyone but I’m certain that many of you pictured cute mascots, characters from your favourite anime, or perhaps Lolitas. What you probably didn’t picture is Manchurian skulls piled high in the streets — just one product of Japan’s brutal imperial past. This dichotomy between Japan’s history and Japan’s present image is not an accident. Kawaii culture has been part of a concerted effort to transform the international perception of Japan and Japanese culture. Genius right? I would argue that sinister is more fitting. The lack of accountability for colonisation and imperialism problematises how we perceive the severity of the consequences and implications of such actions.

But First, what exactly is kawaii culture?

If you’ve ever tried to learn any Japanese, you probably know that kawaii is the Japanese word for cute. While it is used in passing as a compliment or a casual description, kawaii culture is something bigger and far more infectious. Kawaii culture was introduced to the world in the 1960s during a period of immense economic development for Japan. It is widely associated with not just cuteness but softness, vulnerability, and shyness. This permeated into many aspects of entertainment, think Hello Kitty, Animal Crossing, and Rilakkuma.

Kawaii culture is also associated with fashion and style. The Lolita aesthetic, which is widely popular in Japan, embraces all things kawaii. The soft pastels and traditional European silhouettes connote daintiness, helplessness, and the epitome of femininity.

But kawaii culture has spread beyond entertainment and fashion. Walking around Japan you begin to notice that cute characters exist on everything including trains, national landmarks such as Tokyo Tower and even government buildings. It’s widely known that prefectures in Japan have their own mascots, one of the most famous being Kumamon, Kumamoto’s mascot, but even Tokyo’s wards have their own mascots. The ward I stayed in was Nerima and we had an adorable mascot named Nerimaru. His face was on everything from building sites to earthquake safety manuals. Putting cute faces on everything was a conscious effort from the Japanese government to inspire compassion and respect not just towards each other but towards their surroundings.

How is this linked to imperialism?

At the end of the Second World War in 1945, Japan had spent almost a century colonising the majority of East and Southeast Asia including Taiwan, Korea, parts of Mainland China, Manchuria, Vietnam and the Philippines. The Japanese imperial flag still causes forms of PTSD for those who lived under the rule of the Dai Nippon Teikoku, or the Empire of Japan. Japan committed many atrocities in these nations that have caused irreversible damage, mainly in terms of the state of Southeast Asian economies and political systems.

So, when Japan is defeated by the U.S. in 1945, albeit with excessive force, its international image is not a pretty picture. Alongside Japan’s ruthlessness as the metropolis of an empire, its position as an ally to the big bad wolf of war crimes, Nazi Germany, resulted in Japan being perceived as a heartless nation that endorses xenophobic, racist, and violent values. Post-war Japan, like many other countries, was also in great economic decline under the occupation of another country. It was in desperate need of the spoils of export-led growth and international trade. Japan’s development in this respect was deemed a miracle and was the kind of growth that had never been seen before.

But underpinning all that success was a re-design of Japan’s international image. Through the use of kawaii culture, Japan was slowly starting to be perceived as the land of all things soft, cute, and cuddly, which was a complete reversal of their image from 1868 to 1945. Whether completely realised or not, this worked wonders in suppressing Japan’s imperialist past. As a subordinate to America, post-war Japan was infantilised and kawaii culture was both a by-product and catalyst of this. It was a means of expression for many artists affected by the economic hardship, but the focus on being cute and youthful portrayed Japanese people as juvenile. Takashi Murakami, an incredibly well known Japanese artist, questions why Japanese culture is so ”enamoured” with being cute and child-like. Taking into account Japan’s desire to forget their history as an oppressor, it seems as though Japanese culture is enamoured with portraying itself as harmless.

Is this even problematic, though?

Short answer, yes. It’s problematic because it invalidates the experiences of those who were victims of the Japanese Empire. Many communities are still suffering as a result of Japanese occupation in their countries during the Second World War in particular. While there’s no way of undoing what has been done, oppressors must be held accountable for their actions rather than evading the negative consequences that come with the fall of an empire, including being perceived as merciless and barbaric.

The road to reparations is a long and arduous one. However, taking responsibility for wrongdoings is a good start. Although it seems harmless in comparison to a concert hall full of people singing ‘Rule Britannia’ every year — Britain, we’re looking at you —, it undermines the severity of the actions of imperialism. At the expense of the quality of life in many East Asian countries, Japan was in a position to strengthen itself internationally and eventually become one of the richest countries in the world. It’s no secret that we all love kawaii culture but the victims of Japanese imperialism deserve better than the face of their suffering being reduced to anthropomorphic animals and girls in pastel dresses.
