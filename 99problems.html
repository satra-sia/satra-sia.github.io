99 problems

How systemic racism demonises rap music

In 2020s last moments, Maryland States highest court ruled in favour of rap lyrics being admissible in court as evidence against a defendant as a result of Lawrence Montague’s alleged involvement in the murder of George Forrester in 2017.

For the first time, the prejudice that has existed in the United States and the United Kingdom against rap music and its creators has been validated by a judicial system. Their argument? Rap lyrics not only reveal an individual’s wrongdoings but encourage those who listen to their music to partake in violent acts and gang activity. But underlying this defence is a sordid history of hatred of rap music fuelled by racial biases against Black communities in Western countries. The notion that rap music instigates gang violence reinforces the centuries-old narrative that Black people, and therefore their art, are inherently violent and allows local and national governments to absolve themselves of the responsibility of tackling the socioeconomic issues that are actually causing gang violence within Black communities.

I’m sure many of you are thinking “but rap lyrics are violent” and you wouldn’t be wrong. Rap lyrics from 2Pac to Nicki Minaj feature violent imagery and in some cases detailed descriptions of criminal or gang activity. But I must ask, how much of the media that we consume features violent imagery? How many Quentin Tarantino films involve extreme violence? How many Shakespeare plays? How many deaths are in George R.R Martin’s Game of Thrones? All of these heralded creators are lovers of violent acts yet they do not experience the same level of scrutiny that rappers do. Why? Because they, like many others, are protected by their Whiteness.

What I mean by this is that these artists are allowed to explore violent themes without their character or influence being called into question. This is because, despite history books filled with events linking White people and violence, the racial hegemony in Western societies does not encourage us to view White people as the aggressor. Black people, however, have been depicted as violent and primitive throughout Western history, which has formed the racial biases that still exist today. While this is expressed in different forms, the principal remains the same — Black people are violent and barbaric and hence must be controlled. In this case, this extends to the music that Black people create.

Many of the conversations sparked from Maryland’s ruling sought to draw comparisons between other genres of music that, despite violent imagery, are not tied to violence within their respective communities. Heavy metal was a genre that stood out to me the most and I thought it necessary to really paint this picture. Metal music features incredibly violent lyrics, the worst of which are far too graphic and sickening to include in this article.

They detail brutal murders, rape, necrophilia, violence against women, infanticide, matricide, and many more horrific things I’m sure you don’t want to picture. Having listened to both metal and rap extensively, I can assure you that Gucci Mane’s Murder for Fun has nothing on Autopsy’s Charred Remains (seriously, don’t listen to it, it’s nauseating). Yet metal lyrics aren’t brought into murder trials as evidence against White defendants nor are they blamed for school shootings or serial killings. The key difference between rap and metal is that metal’s image is overwhelming White. These artists and their audiences are protected by Whiteness, and Black musicians and audiences are not recipients of this same privilege.

Demonising rap music is one link in a long chain of victim-blaming when it comes to the treatment of marginalised communities within Western societies, namely the U.S and the U.K. In these two countries, rap music has been consistently blamed for the existence and development of gang violence in non-White communities. However, when you look at the textbook causes of crime and illicit activity, including gang-related violence, music of any genre is not among them. Instead there is: lack of opportunity, little social mobility, deprivation, poverty, low- quality education, low-quality housing, limited access to credit, and many more socioeconomic issues that disproportionately affect communities of colour and particularly Black communities because of systemic racism. The segregation and ghettoisation of African American people alone has caused a number of the issues listed above. So while violent rap lyrics may be symptomatic and run parallel to gang violence in some cases, the root of the problem has existed long before hip hop, drill, or grime.

In the U.K, drill music videos are persistently removed from YouTube and are publicly blamed for the acceleration in knife crime rates. This creates a situation where individual artists, groups and songs are being held accountable for alarming homicide figures without any concrete data displaying a causal relationship between drill and knife crime. This implies that the issue is internal in these communities, that young Black men in major cities such as London and Birmingham are creating the problem of knife crime independent of the socioeconomic issues they face as a result of their race. This mechanism of blaming the victim or their own predicament allows governments to avoid addressing the underlying issues that are known causes of crime which only perpetuates the issue further.

Ultimately, it’s time that local and national governments realised that scapegoating rap music is counterproductive in reducing gang-related violence in Black communities. But the ruling in Maryland and the stance of U.K officials suggests that this epiphany is a long way off. It seems much more convenient to have these communities internalise the problems that systemic racism has dealt them rather than pull out their wallets and uplift these communities that make the music we all love to listen to.
