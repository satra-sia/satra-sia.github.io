That's Jazz

A Perspective on Racism and White Supremacy in the U.S.

Jazz, at its heart, is a genre of collaboration and the merging of cultures. From the vibrant performances in New Orleans Congo Square to Carnegie Hall, jazz has been steeped in the waters of cultural interactions, changes and discourse in the United States. An integral part of which is racial discourse. By way of its conception, development, and consumption, jazz has gone hand in hand with racial conflicts and triumphs over it and so upon closer inspection is an interesting lens through which we can look at racial discourse in the United States.

Conception

1800s New Orleans, the birthplace of jazz. A city of constant movement and intersections of traders, slave owners, and travellers alike due to its position in the Gulf of Mexico and along the Mississippi River. This unique location resulted in an eclectic mix of people from across the world in the city at any one point. This included slaves from the African continent, the deep south and the Caribbean Islands. It is no secret that slaves made music and danced to keep spirits high but this took on a unique form in New Orleans. Congo Square, now within Louis Armstrong Jazz Park, was the epicentre of song and dance for slaves from all three regions who were permitted by their masters to come together and perform. These are the beginnings of jazz. A genre born out of the enslavement and oppression of Black people across three continents all converging and communicating musically as a result of the shared pain they desperately needed relief from.

Development

At this point, the genre lacked the formal musical knowledge to be considered as anything more than slaves dancing for “massa”, which leads to jazz’s next development. Also living in New Orleans during the 1800s was a racial group called Creoles of Colour who were mixed-race individuals from slave owners, of slave ancestry. These individuals were often classically trained in various musical instruments and despite having black ancestry and being of brown skin tones, did not identify as Black.

consumption

The consumption of jazz from the late 1800s to the present day can tell us a great deal about racism, White supremacy and White privilege in the United States. Jazz, with its fresh, hot, improvisational sound swept across the country. Popularised by touring pianist Jelly Roll Morton in the 1890s, jazz became increasingly intertwined with minstrelsy as this was often the only way Black musicians were able to travel the country. As a Creole of Colour, Morton did not self-identify as Black despite the Jim Crow laws so he had little reservations rising to fame off the humiliation and mockery of Black people. This movement of jazz around the country resulted in many different bands trying their hand at playing jazz, which included all-White bands.

At this point, it is fair to conclude that jazz is a Black genre created by Black people and, whether they chose to recognise it or not, their descendants. However, the first jazz band to be recorded in the United States was the Original Dixieland Jazz Band in 1917 New York. An all-White band. This was no mistake, the producers knew that a new genre of music needed a palatable face. In the early 20th century U.S., this was a White face. The record did phenomenally well but in reality, was more jazz-like than jazz. The music was composed to drive the idea of improvisation without actually using the improvisational techniques of those who birthed the genre. This was an early form of the cultural appropriation that we still see across the West in the modern-day. The U.S. in particular has a history of taking Black and non-White inventions and marketing them as White creations. In the case of jazz, this meant that anyone who couldn’t see live jazz performances in “Blacks only” spaces heard jazz for the first time from White musicians.

Despite the Whitewashing of jazz’s national image, Black musicians, on account of their talent and innovation, still made it to the forefront of jazz culture and performance. These individuals would be your Louis Armstrongs, Count Basies, Sydney Bechets and Duke Ellingtons. Duke Ellington in particular was significant in overcoming the racial barriers erected by both the law and the industry. His middle-class upbringing and formal musical education alongside New York’s lax segregation laws allowed him to navigate White and Black spaces alike. In modern terms, Ellington was a master of code-switching, a mechanism many Black people used and still use to progress through White spaces despite White privilege. He famously played in the Cotton Club, which was an all-White venue that fetishised the primitive Black man working in the cotton fields. The music Ellington played here was worlds apart from the dignified music he recorded and could play elsewhere. It was known as Jungle Style.

But it’s not Duke Ellington who is credited for breaking down these barriers, no. It is Benny Goodman, a White- Jewish man who felt he identified more with African American jazz players than the White audiences as a result of his working-class upbringing and ethnically diverse neighbourhood in Chicago. Goodman bought the arrangements of Black composer Fletcher Henderson and slowly integrated Black musicians into his ensembles. He became a symbol of the genre because of his ability to play the hot, free, improvisational sounds that made the genre so popular, but his White skin made the music marketable and although he credited his fellow musicians who were Black, there is still a sense of injustice to the African American musicians who invented the genre.

As a result of this Whitewashing of jazz music, today there has to be a concerted effort to relearn where jazz came from. The media, particularly the film industry, paints jazz as a sophisticated genre of the artsy, cultured, White upper-middle class rather than the product of Black pain and suffering at the hands of White people. Films such as Whiplash continue to exclude its African American origins. However, the media has also more recently been promoting the Black face of jazz notably with the release of both Soul and Ma Rainey’s Black Bottom this year. But in reality, jazz remains a genre appropriated by the White middle class, torn from its origins and catapulted into the very White spaces it was once excluded from. Looking through the lens of jazz, we can see how racism and White supremacy shaped cultural movements in the U.S. and how U.S. history should not be addressed without considering the effects of systemic racism as it has moulded the nation every step of the way. Jazz is often regarded as truly American music and a national genre. I would say this is true only in its ability to tell a sordid tale of a tumultuous history.
